# The Grammar of Constraint

### A Comparative History and Combinatorial Framework for Judo, Sumo, Jujutsu (Koryū and Brazilian), and Karate

---

Five combat systems — sumo, koryū (classical) jujutsu, judo, Brazilian jiu-jitsu, and the karate family — descend from a single archipelago's martial culture, yet no two of them fight the same way. They punch different targets, grip different fabric, win by different conditions, and stand in arenas of different shapes. This is not incidental variation. Each system is best understood as a small, formally specifiable rule-set — a grammar of legal grips, legal targets, boundary conditions, and win conditions — and the technique repertoire of each art is the *language generated by that grammar* under the selective pressure of real competition between resisting bodies. Change one rule-primitive (ban a grip zone, shrink the arena, remove a strike target, alter the win condition) and the realized technique space reorganizes, often within a single competitive season. This document treats that claim as a working framework, not a settled academic discipline — but it is grounded throughout in a genuine and active field, *notational* or *performance analysis* in combat sports, which has spent four decades quantifying exactly this relationship between rule change and technique frequency.

The five systems also share a second, non-obvious constraint: they were substantially shaped by two islands — the Japanese archipelago and, within it, the once-separate Ryukyu Kingdom (Okinawa) — whose geographic isolation, internal warfare, enforced peace, disarmament policies, and eventual global export produced a natural experiment in how a shared movement vocabulary diverges when the surrounding legal, social, and geographic constraints change. Brazilian jiu-jitsu's emergence after a single Kodokan emissary settled in Belém in 1914 is, in effect, a second, independent trial of that experiment, run on a different continent under different social constraints, and it produced a recognizably different organism from the same genetic stock.

What follows treats each art's documented history in full, then builds the constraint-grammar framework explicitly, then traces the geographic and political conditions that produced the original rule-sets, then compares the systems in structured detail, and finally surveys the competitive and research state of the art as of July 2026.

## Part II — Canonical Histories

### II.1 Sumo: ritual, rice, and the circle

Sumo's own tradition traces its origins into legend: the *Kojiki* (712 CE) and *Nihon Shoki* (720 CE) both record a mythic strength contest between the deities Takemikazuchi and Takeminakata, and early historical *sumai* matches are recorded as court entertainment and harvest-divination ritual from the Nara and Heian periods (8th–12th centuries), performed before the emperor as *sechie-zumō*. The sport's Shinto substrate is still structurally present in the modern *dohyō*: the ring is ritually purified with salt before every bout, a *gyōji* (referee) dressed as a Shinto priest officiates with a war fan (*gunbai*), a shrine-style roof (*tsuriyane*) is suspended above the ring, and to this day women are barred from entering the *dohyō* itself — a restriction rooted in Shinto and Buddhist purity concepts that has occasionally become a live political controversy in modern Japan (for example, when medical personnel who happened to be women were asked to leave the ring during an emergency).

Sumo shifted from court ritual toward popular and eventually professional entertainment across the medieval and early modern periods, with *kanjin-zumō* (temple/shrine-fundraising sumo) becoming a commercial spectacle by the 17th–18th centuries under the Tokugawa shogunate — the same "enforced peace" period, discussed in Part IV, that reshaped jujutsu. The Japan Sumo Association (JSA), the modern governing body, organizes six annual 15-day *honbasho* tournaments (Tokyo in January, May, and September; Osaka in March; Nagoya in July; Fukuoka in November), each producing an updated ranking sheet, the *banzuke*.

The *dohyō* itself is a precisely specified object: a circle of partially buried rice-straw bales (*tawara*) 4.55 m (15 *shaku*) in diameter — enlarged from 13 *shaku* (3.94 m) in a 1931 rule change — set atop a square clay platform roughly 6.7 m on a side and 34–60 cm high (the Ryōgoku Kokugikan's ring was lowered to 55 cm in 2026 for mechanical clearance reasons). Four bales sit slightly outside the circle's true line at the cardinal points — the *tokudawara* ("privileged bales") — a feature that originated purely as rain drainage when tournaments were held outdoors, and which surviving indoors has become a genuine tactical resource: a wrestler being driven backward will maneuver toward a *tokudawara* because the slight extra depth there can be the difference between a loss and a reversal. It is a clean example of a constraint whose origin was environmental and whose afterlife is strategic.

Wrestlers (*rikishi*) wear only the *mawashi*, a long belt of silk or cotton wound repeatedly around the waist and between the legs; grabbing the *mawashi* anywhere around the waist is legal, grabbing the genital area covering is not. The legal-action set is otherwise minimal: open-hand pushing, thrusting, and slapping are permitted; closed-fist striking, kicking, hair-pulling, eye-gouging, and strikes to the groin are *kinjite* (forbidden acts) resulting in disqualification. The Japan Sumo Association has formally recognized 82 winning techniques (*kimarite*) since a standardization in 2001, plus five additional non-technique ways to end a match (such as a wrestler's own unforced collapse, *koshikudake*). In practice, a 2025 analysis of over 15,000 top-division bouts found that five *kimarite* account for roughly 70% of all wins and the top ten for roughly 85% — with the simple frontal push-out, *oshidashi*, alone responsible for roughly a quarter of all victories. That gap between the formally legal technique space (82 named techniques, arbitrarily many more unnamed variations) and the empirically realized distribution (a small number of techniques dominating under real physical and time constraints) is a recurring pattern across every art in this document, and is addressed directly in Part III.

### II.2 Koryū Jujutsu: the armored root

The oldest well-documented jujutsu tradition is **Takenouchi-ryū**, founded in 1532 by Takenouchi Hisamori in Mimasaka Province. It is a complete system, not a grappling-only art: its curriculum includes *koshi-no-mawari* (armored grappling techniques), *torite* (arresting/restraint methods), rope-binding (*hojōjutsu*), staff, sword, sword-drawing, and glaive work, alongside its unarmed core. Contemporary and near-contemporary schools such as **Tenjin Shin'yō-ryū** (a fusion of two older lines, notable for striking techniques, *atemi-waza*, and for its historical importance as one of the schools Jigorō Kanō studied before founding judo), **Kitō-ryū** (Kanō's other primary source, emphasizing throwing), **Sekiguchi-ryū**, **Yōshin-ryū**, and **Daitō-ryū Aiki-jūjutsu** (later the source of aikido) fill out what by the mid-Edo period had grown into a documented field of over 700 distinct jujutsu *ryūha*.

A precise historical distinction runs through this material and is worth stating exactly, because it is itself a constraint-driven fact: jujutsu historians generally separate **Sengoku-period** (Warring States, roughly 1467–1568, extending in practical terms to the 1615 pacification) battlefield grappling — *katchū bujutsu* or *yoroi kumiuchi*, "armored grappling" — from **Edo-period** (1603–1868) *suhada bujutsu*, "bare-skin" or plain-clothes fighting. This is not a stylistic preference; it is a direct consequence of a uniform constraint. Against an armored opponent, closed-fist striking and most percussive technique is close to useless — the target is padded and plated — so battlefield jujutsu is dominated by joint locks, throws, weapon-retention and weapon-seizure, and methods for exploiting the gaps in armor (the *kogusoku*, or "minor arms," curricula of schools like Takenouchi-ryū). Once the Edo peace removed armor from ordinary training and dueling, striking (*atemi-waza*) becomes technically viable again and reappears prominently in schools coded to that era, such as Tenjin Shin'yō-ryū. The single variable of "is the opponent wearing plate and lamellar armor" reorganizes an entire technical curriculum — the same causal logic, applied to hard steel instead of a sport rulebook, that governs every comparison in this document.

The Meiji Restoration (1868) removed the social and legal basis for jujutsu's existence at a stroke: the samurai class that had sponsored it was abolished, the 1876 *haitōrei* edict banned the wearing of swords in public, and jujutsu itself acquired a reputation — accurate in some cases — as the province of street brawlers competing aggressively and sometimes dishonestly for paying students in a shrinking market. It was directly out of this crisis that Kanō Jigorō, a practitioner of Tenjin Shin'yō-ryū and Kitō-ryū among others, founded the Kōdōkan in 1882, launching the lineage that becomes the subject of the next section.

### II.3 Judo: engineering a live-resistance grammar

Kanō Jigorō (1860–1938) did not simply rebrand jujutsu. His central technical insight — and the one that makes judo the pivot point of this entire document — was that a rule-set could be engineered specifically to make **randori** (free sparring at full speed and full resistance) survivable. Older jujutsu curricula, built around eye-gouges, small-joint breaks, and strikes to vital points, could be *taught* through kata (pre-arranged forms) but could not safely be *drilled* against a fully resisting opponent going full speed, because the techniques were designed to injure. Kanō's Kōdōkan curriculum solved this by selecting and modifying a technique subset — throws scored to a clean back-fall, pins timed to a countable duration, chokes and joint locks (restricted to the elbow) that could be released the instant an opponent submitted by tapping — such that the full technique could be applied at full speed and full resistance without maiming the training partner. This is a rule-constraint innovation in the fullest sense of this document's thesis: the safety rules did not just make judo a "sport version" of jujutsu, they were the enabling condition for live, high-repetition, resistance-based training to exist at all — and live resistance training is itself a search process over technique space, which is a large part of why judo's throwing and grip-fighting technique generated and refined variations faster than static kata-based koryū curricula once both existed side by side.

The Kōdōkan was founded in 1882 at the Eishō-ji temple in Tokyo with a reported nine students. Kanō drew his early technical base primarily from Tenjin Shin'yō-ryū (for grappling and *atemi*) and Kitō-ryū (for throwing principle), and organized the whole system under two guiding formulas: *seiryoku zen'yō* ("maximum efficient use of energy") and *jita kyōei* ("mutual welfare and benefit"). Judo displaced koryū jujutsu in the Japanese police and education systems by the 1890s, aided by a famous — and disputed in its exact details — series of Kōdōkan-versus-jujutsu tournament victories.

Judo internationalized rapidly through emissaries Kanō deliberately dispatched abroad — Yamashita Yoshitsugu to the United States (demonstrating for Theodore Roosevelt), Mitsuyo Maeda across the Americas and Europe (the subject of the next section), and others to the United Kingdom. It entered the Olympic Games at Tokyo 1964 for men and Barcelona 1992 for women, becoming the first Asian martial art with permanent Olympic status and, in the process, locking in a sport-competition rule trajectory that has been revised at essentially every Olympic cycle since.

**Arena and equipment.** The modern IJF contest area is a square mat, 8×8 m up to 10×10 m for senior international events, bordered by a roughly 1 m red "danger zone" and a safety area at least 3–4 m wide at major championships, laid on individual 1×2 m, 5 cm-thick tatami-style mats meeting strict foam-density standards. The *judogi* is a heavy-weave cotton jacket, trousers, and belt; since 1997 elite competition has used one blue and one white gi specifically to aid television legibility — a rule change made for broadcast reasons that had zero effect on technique but a real effect on spectator comprehension, illustrating that not every rule in these systems is technique-generating; some are purely representational.

**Rules as of the 2025–2028 Olympic cycle.** The IJF substantially revised its refereeing rules after the Paris 2024 Games, phasing them in from the 2025 Paris Grand Slam through the 2025 World Championships (Budapest), then finalizing them for the Los Angeles 2028 qualification period beginning with the 2026 Ulaanbaatar Grand Slam. The headline changes are directly relevant to this document's thesis, because they show the technique-space effect of a rule change happening in real time:

- A third score, **yūko**, was reintroduced (having been abolished in the 2017 cycle), now awarded in *ne-waza* (groundwork) for an *osaekomi* (hold-down/pin) of five seconds, alongside the existing *ippon* and *waza-ari*.
- **Grip legality was loosened**: all jacket grips, and gripping under the belt up to the top of the inner thigh, are now permitted (a *shido* penalty applies only if the grip is used *negatively*, i.e., defensively/stallingly rather than to attack). This is a partial reversal of the widely discussed 2010 ban on classic leg-grab throws (*morote-gari*, *kata-guruma*, *te-guruma*, *kuchiki-taoshi*) with hand contact below the belt — a ban that had already, by the research discussed in Part VI, measurably suppressed an entire throw family at the elite level for over a decade.
- Using the head to initiate or defend a throw is now permitted for seniors and juniors (still penalized at cadet level), reversing an older restriction.
- The "bear hug" grip is legal unless the arms are clasped together around the opponent, which draws a *shido*.
- A newly explicit safety rule renders application of *ushiro-sankaku* (rear triangle) in *ne-waza* an automatic *hansoku-make* (disqualification) if it presents high risk of neck or spinal injury to the opponent — a rule written specifically in response to techniques increasingly imported from *no-gi* grappling and Brazilian jiu-jitsu cross-training, which the sport's own governing literature now explicitly acknowledges as an influence on elite *ne-waza*.

Osaekomi (a pin) run to full *ippon* now requires the position be held for 20 seconds. Certain techniques remain banned outright on safety grounds regardless of era — *kawazu-gake* (a leg-entangling technique), *kani-basami* ("scissor throw," banned after a career-ending knee injury it caused in top-level competition), and *dō-jime* (a body-scissor choke) among them.

### II.4 Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu: a rule-grammar transplanted to a new island of constraint

**Mitsuyo Maeda** (1878–1941), born in Aomori Prefecture, began training under Tsunejirō Tomita — one of Kanō's earliest students — around 1894–1895, and became one of the Kōdōkan's leading groundwork (*ne-waza*) specialists of his generation. In 1904 Kanō sent Maeda abroad as a demonstrator and, unusually for a Kōdōkan man, an active challenge-match competitor: Maeda spent the next decade touring the United States, Britain, continental Europe, and Central and South America, taking on wrestlers, boxers, and *savate* fighters under a variety of rule-sets, and acquiring the ring name **Conde Koma** ("Count Combat"). He arrived in Brazil in November 1914, following a route through El Salvador, Costa Rica, Panama, Peru, Chile, and Argentina, and by 1916–1917 had settled enough in Belém to open a formal academy.

There he met **Gastão Gracie**, a Brazilian businessman of Scottish descent, whose teenage son **Carlos Gracie** began training under Maeda around 1917. A precise, frequently blurred historical point deserves stating with rigor here: at the time, "judo" was not yet the universally standardized term even inside Japan — the Japanese Ministry of Education did not mandate "jūdō" over "jūjutsu" in public-school curricula until 1925 — and Maeda's own art was reported in the Brazilian press, and referred to by Maeda himself, indiscriminately as "jiu-jitsu." Brazilian jiu-jitsu's direct technical lineage is therefore Kōdōkan judo's *ne-waza* curriculum as carried by a single, exceptionally ground-fighting-focused emissary, not an unbroken transmission from pre-Kanō koryū jujutsu — a common claim in popular martial-arts writing that the documentary record does not support.

Carlos passed his training to his younger brothers, notably **Hélio Gracie**, a physically frail child restricted by doctors from strenuous exercise for years, who is credited with adapting the standing-dominant Kōdōkan syllabus toward a leverage-and-timing-dominant ground game he could execute despite his stature and strength deficits. The Gracie brothers' own competitive record accelerates this part of the story: fighting *vale tudo* (no-holds-barred) and challenge matches against other Japanese-immigrant judoka in Brazil — most notably a running rivalry with Kōdōkan black belt and catch wrestler **Geo Omori** — the Gracies were repeatedly thrown by superior Kōdōkan standing technique and, in direct response, invested disproportionately in guard work (fighting effectively from the bottom position), a selection pressure that left a permanent signature on Brazilian jiu-jitsu's technical center of gravity relative to judo's. The first dedicated Gracie academy opened in Rio de Janeiro in 1925.

Brazilian jiu-jitsu reached global prominence when **Royce Gracie**, Hélio's son, won three of the first four Ultimate Fighting Championship tournaments (1993–1994) against markedly larger opponents trained in boxing, kickboxing, wrestling, and other karate styles, demonstrating in an essentially unregulated, cross-style arena that ground control and submission could neutralize both size and striking power — an outcome that then reshaped nearly every other combat sport's training methodology in turn (see Part VI).

**Rule fragmentation as speciation.** Where judo consolidated under a single international federation, BJJ never did, and the resulting rule-plurality is one of the cleanest illustrations of this document's central claim available anywhere in combat sports. The same physical action — a heel hook, for instance — is legal or illegal depending on the *simultaneous* intersection of at least three independent constraint axes:

1. **Uniform** (gi vs. no-gi): under IBJJF rules, heel hooks, knee-reaping, and twisting knee locks are illegal in **gi** competition at every belt level, full stop; the same techniques are legal in **no-gi** competition for adult brown and black belts (a rule change effective January 2021).
2. **Rank** (belt level): IBJJF white, blue, and purple belts — in both gi and no-gi — may apply only a straight ankle lock, and only while turning *away* from the knee being attacked; turning toward the knee is itself a foul, independent of whether a submission is obtained.
3. **Federation**: the ADCC Submission Fighting World Championship (founded 1998, no-gi only) and organizations such as EBI permit heel hooks, knee-reaping, toe holds, and calf slicers at essentially all competitive levels, reflecting a design philosophy — "complete grappling," in ADCC's own framing — that treats leg attacks as coequal with upper-body attacks rather than as a specially dangerous late-curriculum reveal.

A single technique's legality is thus determined by a three-dimensional lookup table (uniform × rank × federation) before a single joint has been touched — a genuinely combinatorial rule object, not a metaphorical one.

### II.5 Karate: two islands' worth of hybridization

Karate's lineage runs through the Ryukyu Kingdom, a polity independent of Japan proper until 1879 — the single most important fact for understanding why karate's history reads so differently from sumo's or jujutsu's, and the subject developed further in Part IV. The three Okinawan kingdoms unified under Chinese imperial backing in 1429; the resulting Ryukyu Kingdom paid tribute to Ming and Qing China and hosted continuous exchange with Fujianese traders and envoys, which is the direct channel through which Chinese *quánfǎ* (boxing) — particularly Fujian White Crane methods — entered the indigenous unarmed art known simply as **te** ("hand"; *tī* in Okinawan). The hybrid produced by this exchange was called **tōde** (唐手, "Tang-[China]-hand"), also rendered *karate-jutsu*.

Two disarmaments compound on each other in Okinawan history and are worth stating precisely because they are, again, constraint events with direct technical consequences. King Shō Shin banned weapons possession among the general populace beginning in 1477; the Satsuma Domain of Japan then conquered the Ryukyu Kingdom in 1609, reducing it to a vassal state and — as an occupying power — reinforced disarmament of the general population even more strictly. Unarmed *te* and improvised farm-implement weapons (*kobudō* — the *bō* staff, *sai*, *tonfa*, *nunchaku*, and similar tools) developed under this double constraint, taught in extreme secrecy within family lines, largely among the *Ryukyuan samurē* (aristocratic) class attached to the royal court at Shuri. The Ryukyu Kingdom's formal annexation as Okinawa Prefecture in 1879 stripped the *samurē* of their remaining privileged status, threatening the art's transmission entirely — a crisis structurally parallel to what jujutsu had faced in mainland Japan a decade earlier, and resolved the same way: institutionalization through public education. **Ankō Itosu** (1831–1915), a Shuri-te master, is credited with getting karate introduced into Okinawan public schools from 1905, and with composing the five *Pinan* (*Heian*) kata specifically as a simplified, safety-oriented teaching syllabus for children — kata that remain foundational across nearly every modern style.

By the turn of the century, Okinawan practice had differentiated by locale into three broad regional traditions, each with a distinct Chinese-influence pathway and distinct successor styles:

- **Shuri-te**, associated with Sōkon "Bushi" Matsumura and his student Itosu, emphasizing speed and linear technique; ancestor of Shōtōkan, Shitō-ryū, and the various Shōrin-ryū lines.
- **Naha-te**, associated with Kanryō Higaonna (1853–1916), who trained directly in Fuzhou, Fujian under a master recorded as "Ryū Ryū Kō," and his student **Chōjun Miyagi** (1888–1953), who refined the system into **Gōjū-ryū** ("hard-soft style"), incorporating the breathing-and-structure kata *Sanchin* and the complementary flowing kata *Tenshō*, explicitly organized around a Taoist hard/soft (*gō/jū*) principle.
- **Tomari-te**, a smaller tradition centered on Tomari village, contributing to several of the same successor styles as Shuri-te.

**Gichin Funakoshi** (1868–1957), trained by both Itosu and Yasutsune Azato, demonstrated karate for Crown Prince (later Emperor) Hirohito in Okinawa in 1921 and at Tokyo's first All-Japan Athletic Exhibition in 1922; he elected to remain in Japan, where his teaching — formalized by students into the Japan Karate Association — became **Shōtōkan**, the most widely practiced style worldwide. **Kenwa Mabuni** (1889–1952), who trained under both Itosu (Shuri-te) and Higaonna (Naha-te), synthesized the two lineages in Osaka from 1928 onward into **Shitō-ryū**. **Hironori Ōtsuka** (1892–1982), already a licensed master of Shindō Yōshin-ryū jujutsu before he began studying under Funakoshi, broke away to found **Wadō-ryū** ("way of harmony"), explicitly and self-consciously fusing karate striking with jujutsu body-shifting, joint-locking, and throwing principle — a style whose very name records a deliberate combination of two different constraint-grammars into one curriculum.

A decisive, precisely dated political event then rewrote the art's name at the level of its written characters. As Japan's relationship with China deteriorated through the Manchurian Incident (1931) toward outright war (from 1937), a Chinese-derived name for a martial art became a domestic liability. At a 1936 meeting of Okinawan masters in Naha — including Miyagi, Yabu, Motobu, Hanashiro, and Kyan — it was agreed to follow Funakoshi's already-established mainland practice and replace the character 唐 ("Tang," i.e., China) with 空 ("empty"): the pronunciation *karate* was unchanged, but the written art became 空手, "empty hand," rather than 唐手, "China hand." The same spoken word now pointed to a different ideograph for reasons that were entirely geopolitical rather than technical — a clean example, alongside the *jūjutsu*→*jūdō* terminology standardization of 1925 discussed above, of politics rewriting a martial art's name without touching its curriculum.

**Kyokushin and the knockdown branch.** **Masutatsu Ōyama** (born Choi Yeong-eui, a Korean-Japanese practitioner; 1923–1994) trained across several existing styles before founding what became **Kyokushin Kaikan**, formally organized as the International Karate Organization in April 1964. Kyokushin pioneered **knockdown karate** rules in the late 1960s: bare-knuckle, full-power contact to the body and legs (including leg kicks and knee strikes), full-power kicks to the head, but — critically — **no closed-fist punches to the face**, and no clinching or grabbing under the basic ruleset. The absence of hand strikes to the head combined with full-power head-kick legality produces a technique distribution almost unrecognizable next to boxing or WKF point karate: heavy leg-kick exchanges, high-volume body punching, and a premium on head-kick range and timing, because the single most efficient human striking tool for the head — the fist, at close range — has been legislated out of the target/weapon combination entirely. Kyokushin's 100-man *kumite* (*hyakunin kumite*) — a challenger fighting 100 consecutive fresh opponents over roughly 2.5–4 hours, completed by only a small number of practitioners historically, including Ōyama himself — is not a competition rule but an endurance rite, illustrating that not every constraint in these systems exists to produce a winner; some exist to produce a demonstrated state of the practitioner.

Kyokushin's knockdown ruleset then speciated further, in real time and in ways still visible today: **Ashihara Karate** and **Enshin Karate** modified the base ruleset to permit one-handed grabbing specifically to execute throws for points; **Shidōkan** allows clinching, throws, and brief follow-up grappling attempts; **Seidōkaikan** relaxed rules toward what eventually became, via a further split, the gloved kickboxing promotion **K-1**. Each variant is a small, documentable perturbation of Kyokushin's original rule-set, and each perturbation is associated with a recognizably different fighting style — a live cladogram of rule-driven speciation happening within a single human generation, discussed further in Part III.

## Part III — Rules as Generative Grammar: How Constraint Produces Combinatorics

### III.1 The model, stated precisely

Treat a combat sport's rulebook as a small set of **primitives**:

- **G** — the set of legal grip/contact zones (where on the body, and through what surface, contact may be initiated and sustained)
- **A** — the arena's boundary topology (its shape, and what happens when a boundary is crossed)
- **W** — the win condition (what state, reached how, ends the contest, and under what time structure)
- **T** — the prohibited-action set (techniques or targets excluded regardless of the above)

A technique is a sequence of legal state-transitions permitted by (G, A, W, T) that moves the match toward a state satisfying W. The **technique space** of an art is the set of all such sequences; the art's *style* is not the technique space itself (which is usually far too large to fully enumerate or even to fully name) but the empirically realized *frequency distribution* over that space once resisting, skilled, motivated competitors search it under real physical and temporal limits.

This framework is a way of organizing the comparison in this document — it is not a claim that a formal discipline called "combat-sport combinatorics" exists as a defined academic field. What *does* exist, and what grounds the claim empirically rather than just analogically, is **notational analysis** (also called *performance analysis* or *time-motion analysis*), an active applied sports-science literature — chiefly in judo, but with growing work in BJJ, wrestling, and karate — that has spent roughly four decades doing exactly what this framework proposes in the abstract: quantitatively tracking how a specific rule change measurably shifts technique frequency, combat duration, penalty rates, and grip-configuration efficiency. Several of its concrete findings are used as worked illustrations below, and its existence is the reason this document treats "rules generate technique distributions" as an empirically supported claim about these arts rather than a purely literary conceit.

### III.2 Arena topology

The shape of the legal area and the *consequence* of leaving it are two independent variables, and arts differ sharply on both:

- **Sumo's circle** has the harshest possible boundary condition: touching the ground outside the *tawara* with any part of the body other than the soles of the feet ends the match immediately, with no partial credit and no possibility of continuing from a disadvantageous position. Combined with a small diameter (4.55 m) and no periphery "safety" concept at all, this boundary condition makes explosive, low-risk, forward-driving technique disproportionately valuable relative to prolonged technical exchange — matches average only a few seconds to (rarely) a couple of minutes, and the single technique most associated with pure explosive driving power, *oshidashi*, accounts for roughly a quarter of all wins. The 1931 enlargement of the ring from 13 to 15 *shaku* was itself a deliberate rule intervention in this exact variable.
- **Judo's square mat** treats the boundary as a soft reset rather than a hard loss: stepping out during standing exchange stops the action (*"mate"*) and restarts it centrally, with a penalty (*shido*) only for *intentionally* exiting to escape a technique. The boundary is therefore a tactical resource for both defense (retreat to force a reset) and offense (drive an opponent toward it to limit their escape options) rather than a lethal hazard, which supports much longer standing exchanges and grip negotiation than sumo's arena permits.
- **BJJ's mat boundary** is weaker still: in most rule-sets, action continues near the boundary and is only interrupted if both competitors' relevant contact points leave the area, and — critically — the entire *ne-waza* game is designed around the assumption that the fight will spend extended, boundary-irrelevant time in a single spot on the floor. Removing the boundary as a live tactical factor is part of what allows multi-minute positional sequences (guard passing, back-taking, scrambling) to be central to the sport rather than marginal to it.
- **Karate's WKF square** (8×8 m + 1 m safety area) sits between these poles: exiting draws a warning (*jōgai*) rather than an instant loss, but the smaller total area relative to the athletes' explosive kicking and blitzing range means ring-cutting and retreat management are a constant tactical layer, distinct from both sumo's lethal boundary and BJJ's largely irrelevant one.

### III.3 Uniform and grip topology

What fabric (if any) covers the body, and what of it may legally be gripped, is arguably the single highest-leverage rule variable in this entire comparison, because it determines whether an entire *class* of control mechanism — friction grip on cloth — exists in the technique space at all.

- **Sumo** offers exactly one legal grip surface, the *mawashi*, against a body that is otherwise bare skin (which is itself hard to control by gripping, being slippery with sweat and offering no fabric purchase). This single-surface constraint is a major reason sumo technique concentrates so heavily on body position, hip drive, and controlled contact rather than the multi-point fabric control seen in gi arts.
- **Judo and gi BJJ** offer a dense, structured grip surface — collar, both sleeves at multiple points, the skirt of the jacket, the pants, the belt — and a large fraction of each sport's technical literature (*kumikata* in judo) concerns the sub-game of establishing a superior grip configuration before a scoring action is even attempted. A 2020 notational-analysis study of Bosnia-Herzegovina national championship judo, for instance, found statistically significant differences in *which* grip configuration (same-side vs. opposite-side) male versus female competitors favored, with measurably different technique-efficiency indices attached to specific throw/grip pairings (e.g., *ippon-seoi-nage* from a same-side grip showing a distinct efficiency index from *harai-goshi* from an opposite-side grip) — a direct, published quantification of the grip-variable's effect on realized technique choice.
- **No-gi grappling** (ADCC-style, MMA, Kyokushin-adjacent hybrids) removes the fabric grip surface entirely, which does not reduce control options so much as *replace* them: underhooks, overhooks, wrist and ankle control, and body-lock clinching become the dominant control mechanisms, and — not coincidentally — leg entanglements and leg locks, which do not depend on fabric grip at all, become disproportionately central to the no-gi technical meta relative to the gi game (see Part VI).
- **Karate's lighter-weave gi** is not, in most rulesets, a legal grip target in the way judogi is: WKF rules specifically restrict throws to a small set of conventional sweep techniques (several of which — *de-ashi-barai*, *ko-uchi-gari* — are literally borrowed judo technique names, a direct terminological trace of judo's influence on karate rule-drafting) and prohibit grabbing below the waist or pivoting a throw above belt level, which is precisely why karate's technical center of gravity stayed almost entirely in striking rather than developing a parallel grappling game of its own once these rules stabilized.

### III.4 Win-condition topology and time structure

How a contest *ends*, and under what clock, shapes risk-taking as directly as any grip rule:

- **All-or-nothing scoring** (sumo's single win condition; judo's *ippon*) rewards decisive, high-commitment technique and *punishes* incrementalism, because a partial advantage that does not culminate in the terminal state may earn nothing.
- **Accumulating-point scoring under a fixed clock** (WKF karate's yūko/waza-ari/ippon ladder to an 8-point lead or time expiry; standard IBJJF points for takedown, pass, mount, back-control, and sweep) rewards steady positional accumulation and allows a leading competitor to play conservatively once ahead — a structural incentive toward defensive point-protection that most rule-committees in these sports have, at one time or another, explicitly tried to counteract.
- **Time-asymmetric scoring** is a rarer and more aggressive design choice, best illustrated by ADCC's rule that the first half of a match awards *no positive points at all* — only submissions count — with points activating only in the second half. This single rule-primitive is a deliberate anti-stalling mechanism: because a competitor cannot "bank" a lead early, both athletes are pushed toward high-risk submission attempts from the opening exchange, a design explicitly credited (see Part VI) with intensifying the sport's leg-lock and rapid-scramble character relative to points-from-the-opening-bell formats.
- **Weight-of-evidence / judged decisions** (WKF kata, sumo's rare *hantei* flag decision, judo's golden-score extensions) exist specifically to resolve the residual cases where the primary win condition was not reached within regulation time, and are, not coincidentally, the parts of each sport most frequently revised by rule committees, because a judged tiebreak is where the formal rule-grammar is weakest at constraining subjective interpretation.

### III.5 Three worked illustrations

**Sumo — a Pareto distribution over 82 legal outputs.** The rule-set permits 82 named *kimarite* (and unlimited unnamed variations within them); analysis of 15,333 top-division bouts shows five techniques accounting for roughly 70% of wins and ten accounting for roughly 85%, with the single technique *oshidashi* alone at roughly 26%. This is precisely the shape a generative-grammar framework predicts: a rule-set can license a large formal technique space while a much smaller, physically dominant subset is what real, high-stakes competition under sumo's specific boundary and time constraints actually selects for.

**Judo — quantified rule-change effects on combat structure.** Systematic review of male Olympic-level judo combat time across four consecutive rule regimes shows average match duration moving from 202.8 seconds (2010 rules) to 304.8 seconds (2011–2012 rules) to 237.4 seconds (2016 rules) to 189.8 seconds (2018–2019 rules), with golden-score (overtime) usage rising from roughly 3% of matches (2013 rules) to roughly 21% (2018–2019 rules). Every one of those four numbers is attached to a documented, dated rule change (removal and reinstatement of the *yūko* score, changes to how accumulated *waza-ari* convert to *ippon*, changes to whether penalties alone can decide a match) — a rare case in sport of a controlled-enough natural experiment that researchers can attribute specific combat-structure shifts to specific, individually named rule edits rather than to generalized "evolution of the sport."

**Brazilian jiu-jitsu — a three-axis legality lattice.** As detailed in II.4, whether a heel hook is a legal technique in a given match is fixed by the product of three independent constraint dimensions (uniform × belt rank × federation), each contributing a binary or few-valued legality switch. Even restricting attention to this single submission family, the realized rule-space is a small combinatorial lattice, and competitors' technical development is demonstrably federation-specific: an athlete who trains primarily for IBJJF gi competition builds a technical profile systematically different from one training primarily for ADCC-style no-gi, not from any difference in talent or coaching quality but purely as a rational response to which branch of the lattice they compete inside.
